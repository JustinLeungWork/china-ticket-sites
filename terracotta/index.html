About the Terracotta Warriors

Discovered by farmers digging a well in 1974, the terracotta army of Emperor Qin Shi Huang is considered one of the most significant archaeological finds of the 20th century. Buried around 210 BCE to guard China's first emperor in the afterlife, the site contains over 8,000 life-size clay soldiers, 130 chariots, and 670 horses — each with an individually modelled face. Three excavation pits are open to the public, the largest of which (Pit 1) contains an estimated 6,000 figures still being unearthed. The museum is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and sees around two million visitors a year.

Planning your visit

Allow 3–4 hours for a thorough visit, or 2 hours if you're short on time (Pit 1 is the must-see). The museum is large and partly outdoors, so comfortable shoes and weather-appropriate clothing matter. The exhibition is well-labelled in English throughout. A live English-speaking guide significantly improves the experience — the historical context isn't obvious from observation alone. Weekday mornings are least crowded; Friday and Saturday afternoons can be very busy. The museum closes for the first two weeks of February around Chinese New Year.

Getting there from Xi'an

  • By tourist bus: Bus 306 departs from Xi'an Railway Station (east side) every 15 minutes from 7:00am. Journey takes 50–60 minutes and costs ¥7. Most convenient option.
  • By metro + bus: Metro Line 9 to Huaqing Chi, then change to a local bus. More complex but avoids tourist bus crowds in peak season.
  • By taxi or DiDi: Around 45 minutes from the city centre. Expect to pay ¥80–120. Easier for groups of 3+.

What to know

  • Original passport required — mandatory for entry, one ticket per passport per day
  • Photography is permitted in all pits (no flash, no tripods)
  • The on-site restaurant is average — eat beforehand or pack snacks
  • The gift shop sells official reproductions; street stalls outside sell lower-quality alternatives
  • Allow extra time at peak season — the security queue can add 20–30 minutes
Inside the museum

Three Excavation Pits + the Mausoleum Park

Your ticket covers both sites — connected by a free shuttle bus. Allow 3–4 hours total.

Pit 1 · 一号坑

The Main Army

14,260 m² holding around 6,000 terracotta soldiers and horses. Discovered in 1974 by farmers digging a well. The largest pit, with infantry and war chariots still being unearthed today.

Pit 2 · 二号坑

Cavalry & Archers

6,000 m² in a distinctive L-shape with 939 figures — crossbow units, cavalry squadrons, and battle chariots in complex mixed formations. Considered the tactical highlight of the site.

Pit 3 · 三号坑

The Command Centre

The smallest pit (520 m²) with 68 figures believed to be army headquarters. Unique among the four pits: never burned, so the original painted colours survive here in better condition than anywhere else.

Lishan Garden · 丽山园 — included, free shuttle

The Emperor's Mausoleum Park

A 50 km² archaeological park surrounding the emperor's actual burial mound. Highlights include the Bronze Chariot Hall — two exquisite bronze carriages cast before 210 BCE at half the size of real horses — and over 180 excavated companion pits. The free shuttle bus runs between the Warriors Museum and Lishan Garden every few minutes. Allow at least 1.5 hours here.
