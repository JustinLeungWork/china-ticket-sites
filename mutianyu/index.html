About Mutianyu Great Wall

Mutianyu is widely regarded as the most visitor-friendly and best-preserved section of the Great Wall, located 73km northeast of central Beijing. Unlike the heavily commercialised Badaling section, Mutianyu offers an experience closer to what the wall actually looked like — forested valleys on both sides, dramatic watchtowers every few hundred metres, and far fewer crowds. The section was originally constructed in the 6th century AD and substantially restored during the Ming dynasty (1368–1644). It stretches 2.25km and includes 22 watchtowers. The cable car, chairlift, and toboggan run make it accessible to most visitors regardless of fitness level.

Best time to visit

Autumn (late September to November) is peak season for a reason — the surrounding forest turns gold and deep red against the ancient stone, making it one of the most photographed landscapes in China. Spring (April–May) is the second-best window: clear skies and cool temperatures before summer heat arrives. Avoid the October Golden Week and May Labour Day holidays if you can — the wall becomes genuinely crowded. Weekday mornings are consistently the quietest. Aim to arrive at opening (8:00am) to get the first cable car up before tour groups arrive.

Getting there from Beijing

  • By private car or taxi: 1.5–2 hours via the Jingcheng Expressway. Most hotels can arrange a driver for ¥300–400 return — reliable and worth it for groups of 3+.
  • By public bus: Bus 916 from Dongzhimen station (Line 2, Exit C) to Huairou North Street, then transfer to bus H23 or a local minibus. Total journey 2–2.5 hours.
  • Shuttle from central Beijing: Several operators (including the Mutianyu scenic area itself) run morning shuttles from Wangfujing for around ¥65 one-way.

What to bring

  • Original passport — mandatory at entry, must match ticket name exactly
  • Comfortable shoes with grip — the steps are steep, uneven, and slippery after rain
  • Water (available on site but expensive) and snacks
  • Layers — the wall is several degrees cooler than Beijing city, especially with wind
  • Cash or Alipay/WeChat Pay for on-site purchases (card not widely accepted at vendors)
  • Power bank — you'll want your phone for photos and the QR code at the gate
